Attempting a tailored linen jacket taught me more about garment construction than any YouTube tutorial ever could.

I've been sewing for about three years, mostly knit fabrics and loose-fitting wovens — the forgiving stuff. But this spring I decided to push myself: a structured jacket in a rust-colored heavy linen.

## Why linen is both perfect and punishing

Linen is beautiful. It presses like a dream, drapes with weight and intention, and the texture has this satisfying depth that cotton just doesn't have. But it also frays aggressively and has zero stretch, which means fitting has to be precise.

I used the [Closet Core Jasika Blazer](https://closetcorepatterns.com) pattern, which has excellent instructions but assumes you know what you're doing with tailoring. Reader, I did not fully know what I was doing.

## What I learned about interfacing

The biggest revelation was how much interfacing changes everything. I'd always used fusible interfacing sparingly — a collar here, a waistband there. But a structured jacket is *mostly* interfacing. The chest piece, the under-collar, the front facing...

Sew-in hair canvas behaves completely differently from fusible. It has a bias that you work with, not against. Pad-stitching the lapels by hand took two evenings and I now completely understand why bespoke tailoring costs what it costs.

## The bound buttonholes saga

I made four bound buttonholes. The first was terrible. The second was okay. The third was pretty good. The fourth I'm genuinely proud of.

There's probably a metaphor here about learning curves and scientific method but I'll let you draw your own conclusions.

## Would I do it again?

Absolutely yes. The jacket fits well, looks intentional, and taught me skills I'll use forever. Next up: wool crepe trousers.
