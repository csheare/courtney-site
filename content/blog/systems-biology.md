Systems biology isn't just biology with math sprinkled on top — it's a fundamentally different way of asking questions about life.

When I started my PhD, I thought I knew what I was getting into: build models, run simulations, publish papers. But within the first few months I realized systems biology is really a *philosophy* about how to study complexity.

## The reductionist trap

Classical biology has been incredibly successful by being reductionist — isolate a gene, knock it out, see what breaks. This approach gave us the central dogma, the genetic code, CRISPR. It works.

But it has limits. A cell isn't just a collection of parts. It's an emergent system where the *relationships* between parts matter as much as the parts themselves. A protein doesn't just have a function; it has a function *in context*.

## Networks change everything

When you look at biological systems as networks — protein-protein interactions, gene regulatory networks, metabolic pathways — you start to see patterns that are invisible at the single-gene level. Hubs. Modularity. Robustness.

This is what drew me in: the idea that we can use network structure to make predictions, not just descriptions.

## What I'm working on now

My current work sits at the intersection of machine learning and experimental design. The question I keep coming back to: *how do we optimally learn about a biological system when experiments are expensive?*

Active learning, Bayesian optimization, and multi-modal data integration are all part of my toolkit. But the biological intuition has to drive the modeling choices — not the other way around.
